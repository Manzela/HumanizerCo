# HumanizerCo — Assignment #2: three A/B tests I'd run before scaling spend

*Daniel Manzela · Assignment #2, reasoning write-up*

The campaign is a sponsored Google Search ad on **"free ai humanizer."** Everyone who lands has already typed those words, so they arrive *pre-qualified and impatient* — the page doesn't have to sell the category, it has to not break the promise the ad made and get them to the one action that scores: a signup. CVR = signups ÷ visits, and the only honest leading indicator of it is the click on the primary action (Humanize), then the click that completes the gate. So I picked three bets that sit on that exact path: one **at the top** — the headline that decides whether a qualified click becomes an engaged session — and two **at the conversion moment inside the signup gate**, which answer the two questions a user actually asks at the wall: *is it worth it?* (the offer / value-prop) and *how hard is it?* (the auth friction). I deliberately **dropped a CTA-wording micro-copy test** (instruction → first-person "my") that an earlier draft carried: it reaches the smallest population (only gate-viewers on the email path), pulls the lowest-amplitude lever (one button verb), rests on a single much-overstated 2013 case, and is mechanically **entangled** with the auth-order test — which demotes the very button it would re-word. The gate's *offer copy* occupies the same conversion moment with strictly larger reach and a higher-amplitude lever (motivation, not verb), so it takes the slot instead. I still deprioritized the *free-cap economics* (the 250-word limit) — genuinely high-ceiling but two-sided, since tightening "free" risks the exact promise the ad just made — and queue it next. I also did **not** test things with little headroom: the form is already a single field, the tool clears the fold, and borrowed "social proof" (logo walls, review stars) transfers poorly to a *free* tool where the visitor risks nothing. Quality of bets over quantity — each variant changes exactly one thing.

### Test 1 — Headline: is the bold "100% Human" claim worth its risk? (highest leverage)
For scent-driven search traffic the headline is the single highest-*leverage* element on the page: it's the first thing a qualified click sees, and it decides bounce-vs-engage *before* anything else gets a vote. I've made the **honest** headline the live control — **"Free AI Humanizer — Make AI Text Read Like You Wrote It"** — because the old absolute **"…Sound 100% Human"** contradicts the page's own disclaimers and is avoidable claim-risk on paid traffic. Test 1 then asks the real growth question: **does the bolder absolute claim buy enough conversion to be worth that risk?** The variant restores **"…Sound 100% Human"**, keeping the **"Free AI Humanizer"** scent word-for-word either way, so the only thing that moves is the strength of the promise. The sign is genuinely two-sided and the most contestable of my three bets: this keyword's audience splits between **detector-evasion intent** (for whom the bold "100% human" promise may scent-match *better* and convert harder) and **overclaim-wary intent** (for whom the believable control earns more trust) — the test resolves which pull dominates, and it's the highest-*leverage* of the three because the headline gates the entire funnel. *Isolation note: the variant changes **only** the visible `<h1>`; the `<title>` is held identical to the control, so exactly one on-page element differs. The page title isn't part of the on-page funnel — in a paid Search campaign the SERP headline is the ad, not the page `<title>` — so holding it constant is the stricter isolation, not a weaker one.* **KPIs:** the decision metric is **Signup CVR** (signups ÷ exposures); the most direct *leading* signal for a headline is **bounce / engaged-session rate**, because a scent or trust break shows up first as people leaving before they touch the tool — read together with **Humanize CTR** (first-humanize ÷ exposures), which I treat as a *secondary* read here precisely because the tool sits above the fold and gets used somewhat regardless of the headline. If the bold claim wins CVR *and* holds bounce, it earns its place; if it lifts nothing, we keep the honest control and the claim-risk stays retired.

### Test 2 — Gate offer: trade the one-line pitch for a concrete "what you unlock"
The gate is where the account is actually won, yet its entire case for signing up is currently a single sentence ("Sign up free to run Strong mode and lift the 250-word limit"). At the precise moment a user weighs handing over an email, the page under-sells what's behind the wall — and a styled benefits list already sits in the CSS, unused. The variant turns that thin line into a scannable **value stack**: **Strong mode — our deepest rewrite pass · No 250-word limit — humanize full essays in one pass · Free forever — no credit card, ever.** The hypothesis: making the incremental value *and* the no-cost / no-commitment reassurance explicit at the decision point lifts the share of gate-viewers who complete signup — it pulls on **motivation**, the highest-amplitude lever available inside the modal, and it touches the **whole** gate population regardless of which auth path they take (unlike a button-verb tweak, which only reaches email-path clickers). It stays strictly claim-safe: it advertises *features unlocked*, never an efficacy or detection-evasion outcome. I chose it over the first-person CTA micro-copy precisely because that bet was the lowest-reach, lowest-amplitude, most evidence-thin of the candidates and overlapped this very modal. **KPIs:** the decision metric is **gate→signup completion** (signups ÷ gate-views), *not* CVR over all visits — this copy is seen only by users who reached the modal, so measuring against total traffic would drown the effect in people who never saw it; **Signup CVR** is the confirming outcome. I'd watch the **gate dismiss/close rate** as a guardrail (a longer modal could add its own friction) and hold the result directional until it clears the sample math.

### Test 3 — Auth friction: lead with Google sign-in, not the email field
We already offer both email and "Continue with Google," so the lever isn't *adding* social login — it's *leading* with it. Cold search traffic tends to skew mobile (worth confirming from our own device split), and on a phone the gap between a single tap and typing out an email address is real friction arriving at the worst possible moment — right when we're asking for the commitment. The variant promotes **"Continue with Google" to the first, primary position** above the email field (the control leads with email); the single changed variable is which auth path the user meets first. The hypothesis: defaulting attention to the lowest-effort path lifts gate completion for mobile-heavy traffic. **KPIs:** **Signup CVR** is primary, read alongside the **auth-method mix** (already captured in `signup_completed{method}`) to confirm the reorder genuinely shifts behavior toward the social path rather than just reshuffling it — and, importantly, a **downstream activation guardrail** (did those signups come back and humanize again?), because low-friction social login is known to inflate low-intent accounts, and a "win" that's just easier-but-emptier signups isn't a real CVR win.

### How I'd actually run them (methodology, kept honest)
- **Effect sizes, realistically.** Large-scale practice (Kohavi et al.) shows only ~10–15% of experiments move the metric at all and true wins are usually low single digits — so I size for a detectable MDE (not the seductive +20%), and I discount any thin-sample "winner" for winner's-curse before shipping it.
- **Low-traffic engine.** Pre-scale, frequentist fixed-horizon may never reach significance, so I'd use a **Bayesian / sequential** engine (GrowthBook, PostHog, or VWO SmartStats) that reports "probability to beat control" and is peeking-safe — while still enforcing a **≥1–2 week minimum runtime** to absorb weekday and novelty effects (Bayesian is more *decision-useful* here, not statistically magic).
- **Three tests at once.** One pre-registered **primary metric per test (Signup CVR)** with **Benjamini-Hochberg FDR** across the family; secondaries are diagnostic only. Tests 2 and 3 both live in the gate modal but in **non-overlapping regions** — the offer block at the top, the auth order at the bottom — so they're far less entangled than the CTA-wording test I dropped would have been (that one re-worded the very button the auth-order test demotes). I'd still **use orthogonal assignment with an interaction check** to be safe.
- **Trust the readout.** Randomize and analyze at the **visitor** unit (not session — sessions from one person are correlated); fire the variant assignment **before** the hero paints to avoid flicker-driven **SRM** (alarm at split p < 0.001, which invalidates and restarts the test); route any session whose assignment can't be recorded to an **excluded** bucket, never silently into control.
- **The only oracle is our own funnel.** Every external lift number above is a hypothesis-setter, not a forecast — the verdict is our measured visit → humanize → gate → signup funnel on this traffic.
